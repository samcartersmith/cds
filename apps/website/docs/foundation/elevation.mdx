Elevation is the distance along the z-axis or the depth of an interface. By using elevation, we're able
to help users understand the relationship and importance of different components on the screen.

<Image
  srcLight="/img/foundation/elevation/zaxis_light.svg"
  srcDark="/img/foundation/elevation/zaxis_dark.svg"
/>

## Concept

Elevation is an abtract concept, which allows a singular interface for providing depth to an interface. Depth is key for users to understand the priority and relationship of elements.

CDS Elevation is built on the metaphor of a single light source that sits behind the user's head.

In Light Mode, we imagine that there's sun-like light source that casts a shadow on elements. The elements higher in elevation cast larger shadows than elevations lower in elevation.

In Dark Mode, we alter the metaphor slightly. We can't rely on shadows because of the dark backgrounds, and sun-like light source would be unexpected. But instead, we tweak the metaphor to be more about illumination. We imagine there's a less intense light source that illuminates elements. Elements higher in elevation are more illuminated than elements lower in elevation.

CDS's elevation is presented with a combination of visual treatments: shadows, backgrounds (for dark mode only), and strokes.

## Levels

Levels refer to the placement on the z-axis or how far above it is from the background. The lower the level the closer it is to the background, and the higher the level the further it is away from the background.

We support three levels (including zero) in CDS.

Background overlays help users further focus on a task by visually diminishing and preventing interactions with anything behind it.

We recommend adding Background Overlay to elements with an elevation of Level 2.

## Usage

Elevation should always be meaningful and never used for decoration.

### Transitory components

We primarily use elevation to communicate ephemeral components to an interface, such as modals, banners, or toasts.

Do not use elevation for permanent components.

### Grouped interfaces

In larger interfaces, you may want to break the interfaces into groups or cards. For those interfaces, you may use elevation, but please place all cards on level 1.

### Prevent overlapping and collisions

Just like a traffic controller, it's important to not have elements appear on the same elevation in the same position.

Do not let elements collide.

## Designing with Elevation

Because elevation is an abstract concept with multiple visual treatments, design tools may not be set up to support it. To support elevation, we've provided a few aliases to communicate elevation within design and help your engineers understand the intent.

### Color

<Box width="100%" justifyContent="center" spacingVertical={10}>
  <Image src="/img/foundation/elevation/background_aliases_figma.png" maxHeight="448px" />
</Box>

We've included Background, Background - Level 1, and Background - Level 2 to help communicate elevation with the background color changes in Dark Mode. Engineers will just need to set it to background and elevation to `0, 1, 2`.

### Shadows

<Box width="100%" justifyContent="center" spacingVertical={10}>
  <Image src="/img/foundation/elevation/effects_figma.png" maxHeight="448px" />
</Box>

We've included Levels 0, 1, 2 in the Effects style to communicate the shadow changes in elevation.

### Stroke

<Box width="100%" justifyContent="center" spacingVertical={10}>
  <Image src="/img/foundation/elevation/line_figma.png" maxHeight="448px" />
</Box>

When using Level 1, please set the stroke to `Line`.
